WHAT WE DON'T SAY

FADE IN:

INT. HOSPITAL WAITING ROOM - DAY

Beige walls. Plastic chairs. A TV no one is watching.

VIKRAM (58) sits with his hands folded in his lap, staring at the floor. Beside him sits his son KABIR (29), scrolling through his phone, stopping, not really reading anything.

Long silence.

KABIR
You should have called me sooner.

VIKRAM
You were busy.

KABIR
Baba. She's my mother.

VIKRAM
I know who she is.

Silence again. A nurse crosses the room. Both of them watch until she's gone.

KABIR
How long has she been—

VIKRAM
Since Tuesday.

KABIR
It's Friday.

VIKRAM
(quietly)
I know.

Kabir puts his phone away. Looks at his father. Really looks — for the first time in maybe years. Vikram seems smaller than he remembers. Older.

KABIR
Why didn't you call?

VIKRAM
Because when I call, you worry. And when you worry, you fly home. And when you fly home, you look at me like I've done something wrong.

A long beat.

KABIR
I don't look at you like that.

Vikram says nothing. Which is its own answer.

KABIR (CONT'D)
(softer)
I don't mean to.

VIKRAM
I know you don't.

Vikram finally looks at his son. There's no accusation in it. Just tiredness, and something else — something careful, like a man holding a full glass.

VIKRAM (CONT'D)
She asked for you. Yesterday. She said your name in her sleep.

Kabir looks away. Works his jaw.

KABIR
What did she say?

VIKRAM
Just your name. The way she used to call you for dinner.

Kabir's eyes go wet. He doesn't let them spill.

KABIR
I should have come back more.

VIKRAM
Yes.

Not cruel. Just honest.

VIKRAM (CONT'D)
But you're here now.

The TV changes channels by itself. Neither of them notices.

Vikram reaches over — slowly, awkwardly, like a man unused to the gesture — and places his hand briefly on Kabir's arm.

It lasts only a second. He pulls it back.

But Kabir felt it.

KABIR
(barely above a whisper)
I'm sorry, Baba.

VIKRAM
(also barely a whisper)
I know, son.

They sit together, not touching, not speaking. Outside, rain begins to fall.

FADE OUT.
